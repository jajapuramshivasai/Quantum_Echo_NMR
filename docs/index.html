Quantum Echoes for NMR: Probing Spin Topology via Out-of-Time-Order Correlators

Jajapuram Shiva Sai
4th November 2025

Abstract

This report details a quantum simulation protocol for Hamiltonian learning in many-body spin systems, specifically targeting the extraction of molecular structural parameters. We leverage the Out-of-Time-Ordered Correlator (OTOC) as a sensitive probe of information scrambling within a spin network governed by the secular dipolar Hamiltonian. By implementing a time-reversal echo sequence, the protocol isolates structural information encoded in the dipolar coupling constants \(\{D_{ij}\}\). The time-domain OTOC signal is analyzed via Fourier transform to reveal a power spectrum whose peaks correspond to linear combinations of these couplings. We detail the theoretical framework, including the Suzuki-Trotter decomposition for simulating the quantum dynamics, and discuss the significant computational advantage of this quantum approach over classical methods for characterizing complex molecular geometries.

Introduction

A central challenge in molecular physics and Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) is the precise determination of molecular structure. This task often involves measuring long-range inter-atomic distances, which are encoded in the coupling strengths between nuclear spins. In many-spin systems, the dynamics become extraordinarily complex, and traditional spectroscopic signals, which are a form of Time-Ordered Correlators (TOCs), decay rapidly due to both irreversible decoherence and reversible information scrambling. This rapid signal loss obscures the very structural information one aims to extract.

This report explores a quantum protocol designed to overcome this limitation. By employing a time-reversal echo sequence known as the Out-of-Time-Ordered Correlator (OTOC), we can effectively "unscramble" the quantum information and isolate the coherent dynamics governed by the system's Hamiltonian. The OTOC serves as a powerful tool to probe how a local perturbation spreads throughout a quantum system, making it highly sensitive to the underlying network of spin-spin interactions. Our goal is to demonstrate that the OTOC signal can be used for Hamiltonian learningâ€”that is, to reverse-engineer the dipolar coupling constants \(\{D_{ij}\}\) and, by extension, the molecular geometry.

Physical Model

The Secular Dipolar Hamiltonian

We consider a system of \(N\) spin-1/2 particles (e.g., protons) in a strong external magnetic field and a partially aligned medium, such as a nematic liquid crystal. In the rotating frame and under the secular approximation, the dominant interaction is the through-space dipolar coupling. The system's dynamics are governed by the secular dipolar Hamiltonian:

\[ H_{\mathrm{dip}} = \sum_{i < j} D_{ij} \left( 2 I_{z,i} I_{z,j} - I_{x,i} I_{x,j} - I_{y,i} I_{y,j} \right) \]

where \(\mathbf{I}_k = (I_{x,k}, I_{y,k}, I_{z,k})\) are the spin operators for the \(k\)-th particle, and \(D_{ij}\) is the residual dipolar coupling (RDC) constant between spins \(i\) and \(j\).

Residual Dipolar Couplings

The RDC is the key parameter that links the quantum dynamics to the molecular geometry. It is given by:

\[ D_{ij} = -\frac{\mu_0 \gamma^2 \hbar}{4\pi r_{ij}^3} S \frac{1}{2} (3\cos^2\theta_{ij} - 1) \]

where \(\gamma\) is the gyromagnetic ratio, \(r_{ij}\) is the inter-spin distance, \(\theta_{ij}\) is the angle of the inter-spin vector with respect to the external magnetic field's director, and \(S\) is the Saupe order parameter of the medium. Since \(D_{ij} \propto r_{ij}^{-3}\), a precise measurement of the coupling constants provides a direct path to determining the molecular structure.

The Out-of-Time-Ordered Correlator (OTOC)

Definition and Fidelity

The OTOC is a four-point correlation function that measures the degree of non-commutativity between two operators, one of which has evolved in time. For two unitary operators \(W\) and \(V\), the OTOC is defined as:

\[ F(t) = \langle \psi | W^\dagger(t) V^\dagger W(t) V | \psi \rangle \]

where \(W(t) = U^\dagger(t) W U(t)\) is the operator \(W\) in the Heisenberg picture, with \(U(t) = e^{-iHt}\) being the time-evolution operator. The decay of the real part of \(F(t)\) from unity is related to the growth of the squared commutator, which quantifies the spread of information (scrambling):

\[ \Re[F(t)] = 1 - \frac{1}{2} \langle [W(t), V]^\dagger [W(t), V] \rangle \]

The Quantum Echo Protocol

The OTOC can be measured experimentally using a quantum echo protocol, which consists of the following steps:

  1. Initialization: Prepare the system in an initial state \(|\psi_0\rangle\).
  2. Forward Evolution: Evolve the system forward in time for a duration \(t\) with the Hamiltonian \(H\), i.e., apply \(U(t)\).
  3. Perturbation: Apply a local unitary operator \(V\), known as the "butterfly" operator.
  4. Backward Evolution: Evolve the system backward in time for the same duration \(t\), i.e., apply \(U^\dagger(t)\).
  5. Measurement: Measure the expectation value of a local observable \(W\).

The final measurement yields the OTOC. This sequence is powerful because the backward evolution reverses all dynamics that commute with \(V\), isolating the effect of the perturbation.

Numerical Implementation

Suzuki-Trotter Decomposition

Simulating the time-evolution operator \(U(t) = e^{-iHt}\) is challenging because the terms in the Hamiltonian \(H = \sum_{k} H_k\) do not generally commute. The Suzuki-Trotter formalism provides a way to approximate the evolution by breaking it into a sequence of smaller, manageable steps.

The first-order Lie-Trotter formula approximates the evolution over a small time step \(\Delta t\) as:

\[ e^{-iH\Delta t} = e^{-i\sum_{k} H_k \Delta t} \approx \prod_k e^{-iH_k \Delta t} + \mathcal{O}(\Delta t^2) \]

The total evolution to time \(t = n \Delta t\) is then approximated by repeating this step \(n\) times. The global error of this approximation is \(\mathcal{O}(t^2/n)\). For the dipolar Hamiltonian, where \(H = \sum_{i<j} H_{ij}\), this becomes:

\[ U(t) \approx \left( \prod_{i<j} e^{-iH_{ij}\Delta t} \right)^n \]

Each term in the product, \(e^{-iH_{ij}\Delta t}\), represents the evolution under a single two-spin interaction. This can be further decomposed into a sequence of two-qubit quantum gates. Since the components of \(H_{ij}\) (i.e., \(I_{z,i}I_{z,j}\), \(I_{x,i}I_{x,j}\), and \(I_{y,i}I_{y,j}\)) do not commute with each other, we must apply the Trotter approximation again for each pairwise term:

\[ e^{-iH_{ij}\Delta t} \approx e^{-i D_{ij} (2 I_{z,i} I_{z,j}) \Delta t} \cdot e^{i D_{ij} (I_{x,i} I_{x,j}) \Delta t} \cdot e^{i D_{ij} (I_{y,i} I_{y,j}) \Delta t} \]

Each of these exponentials corresponds to a standard two-qubit rotation gate (e.g., RZZ, RXX, RYY) that can be implemented on a quantum computer.

Higher-Order Formulas

Higher accuracy can be achieved using symmetric decompositions. The second-order Suzuki formula, with a local error of \(\mathcal{O}(\Delta t^3)\), is given by:

\[ S_2(\Delta t) = \left(\prod_{k} e^{-iH_k \Delta t/2}\right) \left(\prod_{k' \text{ in reverse}} e^{-iH_{k'} \Delta t/2}\right) \]

This "forward-backward sweep" cancels the leading-order error term. Suzuki also introduced a recursive construction for arbitrarily high-order formulas. The \(2k\)-th order formula \(S_{2k}(\Delta t)\) is constructed from the \((2k-2)\)-th order formula \(S_{2k-2}(\Delta t)\):

\[ S_{2k}(\Delta t) = \left[S_{2k-2}(p_k \Delta t)\right]^2 S_{2k-2}((1-4p_k)\Delta t) \left[S_{2k-2}(p_k \Delta t)\right]^2 \]

where \(p_k = (4 - 4^{1/(2k-1)})^{-1}\). While computationally more expensive, these higher-order methods allow for larger time steps \(\Delta t\) for a given accuracy.

Measured Observables

In our simulation, we measure two quantities. The first is the OTOC signal itself, where we choose local Pauli operators for \(W\) and \(V\). For instance, \(W = I_{x,m}\) on a measurement spin \(m\) and \(V = I_{x,k}\) on a perturbed spin \(k\).

\[ M_{\mathrm{OTOC}}(t) = \langle \psi_0 | U^{\dagger}(t) V^{\dagger} I_{x,m} U(t) V | \psi_0 \rangle \]

The second is the Loschmidt Echo (LE), which serves as a baseline. It follows the same protocol but with the identity operator for the perturbation (\(V=I\)).

\[ M_{\mathrm{LE}}(t) = \langle \psi_0 | U^{\dagger}(t) I_{x,m} U(t) | \psi_0 \rangle \]

The difference signal, \(\Delta M(t) = M_{\mathrm{LE}}(t) - M_{\mathrm{OTOC}}(t)\), isolates the dynamics resulting from the scrambling process and is particularly sensitive to the coupling pathways between the perturbed and measured spins.

Data Analysis

Spectral Extraction of Couplings

The time-domain signals \(M_{\mathrm{OTOC}}(t)\) and \(\Delta M(t)\) contain oscillations at frequencies determined by the energy eigenvalues of the Hamiltonian \(H\). Since these eigenvalues are functions of the coupling constants \(\{D_{ij}\}\), the frequencies in the signal are directly related to the molecular structure.

To extract these frequencies, we compute the power spectrum of the time-domain signal using the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT):

\[ \text{Spectrum}(\omega) = \left| \mathcal{F}\{M_{\mathrm{OTOC}}(t)\} \right|^2 \]

The resulting spectrum exhibits sharp peaks at frequencies \(\{\omega_k\}\) that correspond to linear combinations of the dipolar couplings. By identifying these peak locations, one can construct a system of equations to solve for the unknown \(\{D_{ij}\}\) values, thus performing Hamiltonian learning.

Resolution Limits

The frequency resolution of this spectroscopic method is fundamentally limited by the maximum observation time, \(t_{\max}\), according to the uncertainty principle:

\[ \Delta \omega \ge \frac{2\pi}{t_{\max}} \]
Remark (Sensitivity vs. Coherence Time):
The optimal observation time is determined by a trade-off. A longer \(t_{\max}\) provides better frequency resolution, allowing one to distinguish between closely spaced coupling constants. However, the total observation time is practically limited by the system's intrinsic decoherence time (\(T_2\)), beyond which the quantum signal is lost to environmental noise.

Computational Advantage

Classical Simulation Complexity

Simulating quantum dynamics on a classical computer is exponentially hard. For an \(N\)-spin system, the Hilbert space dimension scales as \(2^N\). Classical simulation of the time evolution requires:

  • Memory: Storing the state vector requires \(\mathcal{O}(2^N)\) complex numbers.
  • Time per step: Matrix-vector multiplication for \(U(t)|\psi\rangle\) requires \(\mathcal{O}((2^N)^2) = \mathcal{O}(4^N)\) operations for a dense Hamiltonian matrix.
  • Total cost: For evolution to time \(t\) with \(n\) time steps, the cost is \(\mathcal{O}(n \cdot 4^N)\).

This exponential scaling limits classical exact simulation to \(N \lesssim 30\) spins on even the most powerful supercomputers.

Quantum Circuit Efficiency

On a quantum computer, the same evolution is implemented using a quantum circuit with resources that scale polynomially with the system size.

  • Qubits: \(N\) physical qubits are required, a linear scaling.
  • Gates per step: Using a Suzuki-Trotter decomposition, each time step \(\Delta t\) requires implementing gates for each of the \(\mathcal{O}(N^2)\) pairwise interaction terms. The number of gates per term is constant. Thus, the gate count per step is \(\mathcal{O}(N^2)\).
  • Total cost: The total number of gates to simulate to time \(t\) is \(\mathcal{O}(n \cdot N^2)\).

This polynomial scaling represents an exponential speedup over classical simulation, making the characterization of large, complex molecules feasible on future quantum hardware.

Conclusion and Future Directions

We have outlined a quantum protocol based on the Out-of-Time-Ordered Correlator that serves as a novel spectroscopic tool for Hamiltonian learning in interacting spin systems. The method leverages the sensitivity of information scrambling to the underlying coupling network, allowing for the extraction of structural parameters from a spectral analysis of the OTOC signal. The inherent robustness of the echo sequence and the exponential advantage of quantum simulation make this a promising approach for characterizing molecular structures that are intractable for classical methods.

Future work will focus on several key areas:

  1. Hardware Implementation: Deploying the simulated Trotter circuits on existing NISQ (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum) hardware to validate the protocol experimentally.
  2. Error Mitigation: Developing and integrating advanced error mitigation techniques to suppress the effects of decoherence and gate errors on real quantum devices.
  3. Inverse Problem: Creating robust computational algorithms, potentially based on machine learning, to solve the inverse problem of mapping the observed spectral peaks back to a unique set of inter-atomic distances.
  4. Scaling: Extending the simulations and theoretical analysis to larger and more complex molecules, such as biological macromolecules, to assess the protocol's performance at scale.
